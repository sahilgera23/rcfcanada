IN THE MEMORY OF FAHIM

Fahim Ataullah-Jan developed his love of sports while studying at Burn Hall, a prestigious boarding school in Pakistan. He enjoyed summer holidays at the family cottage in the mountains playing cricket with his cousins and friends, and hiking with his beloved German shepherd dogs. Even then, he was the life of the party, nicknamed Elvis Presley for dressing like his idol, amusing his friends by pretending to play guitar with a tennis racquet. He also loved Gene Vincent’s Bluejean Bop and Be-Bop-A-Lula. At age 17, tragedy hit. His father died in an air crash, devastating Fahim and the family. Soon after, he joined the Pakistan Military Academy, and after a gruelling three years of training, emerged a polished, promising young officer. He joined the Guides Cavalry, an elite unit of the Armoured Corps. A born leader, Fahim advanced to Tank Squadron Commander. In 1970 he met the love of his life, Najma Hasnain, during her visit to Pakistan from Canada. They married that year and went on to have two daughters, Sabrina and Syra, his prides and joys. Fahim and Najma’s epic love story spanned 42 years. After retiring as a major from the Pakistani military, Fahim and the family emigrated to Canada in 1980.

He embraced his new life with enthusiasm, prepared to do whatever was required to reinvent himself. After stints selling cash registers, coffee machines and computers, he landed a job representing a medical supply company. Soon after, he started his own wholesale watch business, Time and Time Again, which he managed until last December, despite deteriorating health. Fahim played as hard as he worked.

He loved to entertain, and was always the last to leave the dance floor – and the party (sometimes only when Najma dragged him out). He arranged New Year’s Eve and costume parties (often winning first prize), picnics and cricket events. His love of cricket led Fahim to form the Renegades Cricket Fraternity, a team of die-hard cricketers of varied ages in the Greater Toronto Area. As captain, he attended weekly practices and league games without fail, followed by mandatory drinks where conversation flowed and bonds strengthened. His need for portable oxygen did not deter him from playing – he merely ensured the oxygen tube was long enough to enable him to bowl. (A cricket tournament is being planned in Fahim’s name in late August.) Despite being neither British nor Christian, Fahim was invited to join the Imperial Officers Association of Canada, an association of retired British military officers, where he served on the executive and as president (the only non-Caucasian to have the honour).

He was also a founder and president of the Pakistan Armed Forces Association Canada and co-chair and director of the Canada Pakistan Business Council. Wherever he went and whomever he met, Fahim left a lasting impression with his charm, gift of the gab and the ability to make people laugh. Never afraid to shed a tear (of course excusing it as “allergies”), Fahim lived his life as an officer and a gentleman to the fullest. His spirit lives on in all those who had the pleasure of knowing him.

Renegades Cricket Fraternity (RCF) is a social club for cricket enthusiasts. Our fraternity promotes an environment of companionship and brotherhood dedicated to the intellectual, physical, moral, and social development of its members. RCF was formed in 1992 by the Late Fahim Attaullah Jan, an avid cricketer. The first President for RCF was Dr. Saadat Syed, also a cricket aficionado. In 2009 this legacy was passed on to Ajmal Chughtai who is now the Team Manager, Co-ordinator and the Team Captain. Fahim is solely responsible for creating a culture where every new member was welcomed to be part of this wonderful family. A new member would be introduced to the Renegades culture by Fahim himself because he wished for all the members to have the same level of enthusiasm and passion that he was very well known for. Fahim’s passion & love for cricket was so intense that even a severe health condition did not dissuade him from coming to practice for the game he loved. In his last days, Fahim would put an oxygen mask on his mouth and would come to practice and bowl with his usual long run up. Fahim’s legacy is very much alive with a cricket tournament called Fahim Attaullah Jan T-20 Cup organized every year in Markham as a remembrance of his passion for cricket.

In early 90s when this team was formed there were very few organised leagues and cricket shops in Toronto, so anyone having a full cricket kit was welcomed in the team. Initially we used to play friendly games with different teams and then in 2002 RCF joined Markham Cricket association (MCA) and started playing competitive cricket thereafter. RCF has won the league title twice and has qualified every season to play either the semi-final or the final. We have a very unique culture and we are one of the rare teams who have members from different national & cultural backgrounds. Our members come from countries such as Pakistan, India, Srilanka, Bangladesh and England. We are truly multicultural and always welcome cricketers irrespective of their background, culture or race.

We practice indoor cricket every Wednesday evening throughout the year followed by drinks and food. This is not just a weekly practice but a tradition from the first day RCF was formed. Besides cricket this tradition allows everyone to have some relaxed quality time to chat and get to know each other. We also have an annual team dinner in which all the families get together, celebrate our cricket season and award the best performers of the year.

CATCH US SWEATING IT OUT

Nestled in the heart of Markham, these two grounds are the home of cricket in the MCA. RCF has had many a notable performance on these two beautiful outfields. Games of 35 and 20 overs are played during the months of May to September starting as early as 9 am. During the Annual T20 tournament, food and drinks are arranged by the MCA to fuel the teams as they fight it on on the pitch.
